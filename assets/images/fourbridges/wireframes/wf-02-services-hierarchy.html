Why process-first fails Starting with "Our Process" puts the advisory firm's operations before the client's problem. Founders who have worked with investment banks cite "I never understood what I was paying for" as the most common frustration. A process-first layout requires the reader to translate advisory activities into personal outcomes — cognitive work that creates friction at highest-stakes evaluation moments.
Why outcome-first works Leading with "What You Achieve" creates immediate relevance — the visitor recognises their problem and the resolution before reading a single process step. When process detail follows, it reads as evidence of how the outcome is delivered, not a description of the service. The "Ideal For" qualifier provides self-selection without exclusionary framing. Process becomes proof, not preamble.
How this translated to final UI
Every service section on the live site opens with "What You Achieve" as the primary heading
Process steps appear below, visually de-emphasised as supporting evidence
"Ideal For" tags at base of each card allow self-qualification without exclusion language
Dark card header for outcomes makes the benefit visually distinct from the mechanism
What this decision improves
Clarity: Visitor immediately knows if this service solves their problem
Conversion: Reduced friction — no translation required from process to value
Credibility: Process detail reads as capability proof, not sales copy
Self-qualification: "Ideal For" routes correct mandates without gatekeeping
