Why credentials-only fails A credentials list — Enam, Axis Capital, 20 years — signals capability but not differentiation. In testing, this version consistently landed as "impressive but indistinguishable." Every major advisory firm can produce a comparable résumé. The credentials establish a floor but provide no thesis for why this firm is structurally different. The reader is left to infer motivation, which produces uncertainty, not trust.
Why the hybrid structure works The two-layer structure is the key invention. Layer 1 (institutional credentials) answers the "is this person qualified?" question quickly. Layer 2 (conviction to leave) answers the more important question: "why is this person doing this independently, and why does that matter to me?" The departure decision reframes independence as conviction — the founder left not because he had to, but because he saw something others didn't. Biography becomes evidence of alignment, not just capability.
How this translated to final UI
About page opens with credentials laid out as a visual timeline, not a list
The departure decision given a full typographic moment — not buried in a paragraph
"He eventually made the decision to build something of his own" is the thesis sentence — set at large scale
Client profile described as "who they are" (identity), not "what they need" (functional request)
What this decision improves
Trust: Founder has "skin in the game" — alignment precedes capability
Differentiation: No competitor can replicate the conviction narrative
Credibility: Institutional floor prevents the conviction from reading as naïveté
Client resonance: Founders who take bold decisions recognise the pattern
