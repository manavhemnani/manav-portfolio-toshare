Why flat type fails in this context Financial services sites that rely solely on copy tone for personality produce a consistent result in testing: the site feels professional but cold. Without a visual rhythm device, headings across pages feel interchangeable. Founders who experienced advisory before describe these sites as "all the same." The sans-serif uniformity signals corporate, not conviction.
Why systematic italic works Serif italic is native to editorial and analytical writing — the FT, the Economist, longform financial commentary. It signals thought, not decoration. Applied systematically to emotionally significant phrases (your, advisory, conversation, something of his own), it creates a brand rhythm that accumulates as personality without signalling effort. The device was invisible to most readers consciously — but in testing, the version without it felt "colder."
How this translated to final UI
Cormorant Garamond loaded as the serif italic font — editorial, not decorative
Every major heading sitewide has exactly one phrase in serif italic
No icons, no illustrations, no lifestyle photography — type carries all personality
One exception: founder portrait on About page, which replaces the need for illustration
What this decision improves
Brand personality: Distinctive rhythm without added visual complexity
Trust signals: Editorial serif suggests considered, analytical voice
Warmth: "colder" perception eliminated without adding colour or illustration
Consistency: One rule applied everywhere = recognisable system, not ad-hoc choices
