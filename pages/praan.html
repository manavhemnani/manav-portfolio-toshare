—01

The Problem

The brief wasn't a UI problem. It was a belief problem. Air quality is invisible — people don't see it, don't feel it, and rarely think about it until something is already wrong. Praan builds patent-pending, filterless air purification technology deployed across factories and large industrial spaces. Technically, it's impressive. But technically impressive is exactly the wrong place to start with a buyer who doesn't yet believe they have a problem worth solving.

"How do you make people care about something they can't see — and then trust a solution they can't fully understand?"

The question that shaped every major decision in this project

The design challenge wasn't simplifying deep tech — it was making it aspirational, relatable, and believable across three very different audiences: factory operators who needed compliance reassurance, investors who needed to see a credible business, and engineers who needed technical confidence. A single digital ecosystem — website, motion language, and product experience — had to carry all of that simultaneously, without collapsing under the weight of trying to speak to everyone.

—02

Constraints

Target users 35–55 — low IoT literacy, onboarding had to be zero-effort ₹80,000 price point — digital presence must match hardware credibility New product category in India — no existing mental model to build on App + website must share a coherent visual and motion language

At ₹80,000 — an aspirational price for a product category with no established mental model in India — the website couldn't just explain Praan. It had to earn the right to ask for that much. Every design decision was weighed against a single question: does this signal a brand that deserves this price, or one that's still justifying it? That pressure ruled out feature density, promotional language, and anything that felt like it was trying too hard. Restraint wasn't a stylistic choice. It was the only credible one.

—03

Context & Team

I joined Praan in February 2023 as a full-stack designer, working directly under the founder for four months. The starting brief was a website redesign — but the scope expanded quickly to cover everything the brand touched digitally: information architecture, content hierarchy, high-fidelity UI, motion language, and visual storytelling for investor and stakeholder communication. I also mentored a junior designer on digital asset development throughout the engagement.

—04

What Didn't Work

The pre-engagement site led with product specifications — PM2.5 filtration percentages, airflow volumes, certification badges — before establishing any reason for a visitor to care. Navigation was flat: Home, About, Technology, News, Careers, Contact. There was no hierarchy between commercial and technical depth, and no single audience owned the primary narrative. Every page tried to speak to everyone simultaneously. The result was a site that felt thorough and trusted nothing.

One front door. Three audiences. Different depths.

Both directions — spec-density and audience segmentation — failed for the same underlying reason: they prioritised the brand's need to demonstrate thoroughness over the visitor's need to feel something first. A spec-dense page reads as defensive; an audience selector reads as unsure. The alternative was harder to design but more confident to experience: one narrative that started with the universal outcome — cleaner air, healthier spaces — and made deeper content available to whoever wanted it. Operators, investors, and engineers all entered the same front door. They left by different routes. None of them were ever asked to declare who they were at the threshold.

—05

Key Decisions

▶ How do you explain a mechanism that works invisibly — static photography or custom motion?
Options considered
  • High-resolution product photography — device in situ, lifestyle contexts
  • Side-by-side comparison diagrams — before/after PM2.5 readings with filter vs filterless
  • Text-heavy technical section — filterless mechanism explained through copy and diagrams
  • Custom motion animation — show the mechanism working in real time, 10–15 seconds
What we chose
  • Custom filterless technology animation as the primary explanation mechanism
  • One 12-second loop replaced approximately 200 words of technical copy
  • Photography and written specs repositioned to supporting layer below the fold
Why: The filterless mechanism — removing particulates using electrostatic fields, without a physical filter — is genuinely counterintuitive. "No filter" sounds like a claim that needs to be earned. Static photography of the device proves it exists; it proves nothing about how it works. A 12-second animation lets a sceptical buyer watch the mechanism operate and arrive at their own conclusion. That shift — from persuasion to demonstration — changed the credibility pathway entirely. Motion isn't a stylistic choice here. It's the only format that closes the proof gap.
⚡ Website hero: lead with product specifications or lead with human outcome?
Options considered
  • Tech-forward: HEPA grade, PM2.5 specs, certifications
  • Lifestyle-forward: family imagery, clean home feeling
  • Problem-forward: Delhi pollution data, health risk framing
  • Outcome-forward: the result, simply stated
What we chose
  • Outcome-forward hero — the result, not the mechanism
  • Technical specs present but never leading
  • Trust built through restraint, not feature density
Why: At ₹80,000, the customer has already decided air quality matters. The website's job isn't to justify the problem — it's to make them trust Praan as the answer. Leading with specifications reads as defensive. A brand that doesn't need to list every feature to earn attention has already won a positioning battle. Restraint, at this price point, signals confidence.
⚡ How do you design one information architecture to serve three audiences — without a separate website for each?
Options considered
  • Separate landing pages per audience — operator / investor / engineer
  • Audience selector modal at entry ("I am a…")
  • Layered IA: single navigation with depth-gated content sections
  • One narrative, universally legible, with role-specific depth nested below
What we chose
  • Single entry point — no audience segmentation at arrival
  • Primary narrative speaks to the universal outcome: clean air, healthier spaces
  • Technical and commercial depth available in nested sections — never gated, never leading
Why: Three separate websites would fragment an already early-stage brand. An audience selector modal signals the brand doesn't know who it is — if you have to ask who I am before you talk to me, you've already lost me. One coherent narrative starting with the universal outcome (cleaner air) and expanding into depth made the brand feel confident rather than over-targeted. Operators, investors, and engineers all enter the same front door. They find what they need by going deeper — not by being routed separately.
—06

The Ecosystem

The website, motion language, and HIVE product experience were designed as one system — not three separate deliverables. The website persuades the prospect; the HIVE app reassures the owner day-to-day; the motion language connects both and makes them feel like the same brand at different distances. Visual principles that governed the website's restraint — ambient over explicit, outcome before specification, depth available on demand — carried directly into the app's home screen logic and the product's animation design. The result is that a customer moving from the marketing site to the product interface encounters the same signature: unhurried, confident, and technically precise only once that confidence has already been earned.

Key Learning

Users don't need less information — they need information delivered at the right moment, in the right order, at the right level of confidence. Layered disclosure — ambient status first, precision data on demand — is a pattern I'd apply to any IoT, health, or environmental product facing the same tension between completeness and clarity.

—08

Reflection

What worked

Working directly under a founder — rather than through the approval layers of a studio — created a fundamentally different kind of ownership. Every IA decision, motion direction, and content call was trusted and shipped as proposed. That accountability sharpened judgment quickly. The iteration loops were structured enough to be productive, and adaptable enough to absorb real engineering and situational changes without derailing the work. It's a way of working I'd choose again for early-stage product design.

What I'd do differently

The hardest thing about this project — and the thing I underestimated — was reviewing the work through lenses that weren't mine. Investors, engineers, and factory operators all process credibility differently, and in a fast-paced founder-led environment, there wasn't always time for deep perspective-shifting. I'd build lighter, faster feedback cycles into the process earlier: shorter stakeholder check-ins at each milestone rather than waiting for full presentation reviews. Designing for multiple audiences in parallel requires more frequent reality checks than designing for one.
